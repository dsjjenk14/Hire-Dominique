Why It Makes Sense

Why it makes sense to bring me in-house.

Most firm-side recruiters have only ever seen one side of the process. I have worked both. I also understand NALP guidelines and how OCI fits into a firm's broader hiring calendar.

🔄
I have worked both sides
I spent years on the agency side. I know how external recruiters work a search and where most cut corners. I also understand NALP timelines, OCI calendar pressure, and what it looks like when a firm's recruiting process is running behind. That perspective saves time and money.
🤝
Partners will actually like working with me
I keep communication tight, I do not waste time, and I respect that partners are billing by the hour. My job is to make their hiring process easier, not add to their workload.
🏛️
I understand what it looks like from the inside
I have had direct conversations with recruiting directors and hiring partners at DC firms about how they structure their search process, what breaks down at the offer stage, and what they wish outside recruiters understood. I am not guessing at what firm-side work looks like. I have been asking about it deliberately.
📍
I know the DC legal market specifically
DC is not a generic legal market. The lateral pipeline here runs through government service, the AG track, and federal clerkships in ways that do not exist in New York or Chicago. Government attorneys make up a significant part of the lateral candidate pool. I source with that in mind and have built pipelines that reflect it.
🌱
Recruiting is a long game and I play it that way
The associate I place today is a partner candidate in ten years. I stay in contact with the people I place, follow their careers, and reach back out when something relevant comes up. Most agency recruiters move on after the fee clears. I do not work that way.
